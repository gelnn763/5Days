⚖️ The laws that protect you
5Days is grounded in these specific laws. Here's what they say.
Chicago Residential Landlord and Tenant Ordinance
CHICAGO MUNICIPAL CODE · CHAPTER 5-12
The RLTO is one of the strongest tenant protection laws in the country. It applies to most residential rentals in Chicago and gives tenants rights that significantly exceed Illinois state law — including required notice formats, mandatory disclosures, rent withholding for uninhabitable conditions, and anti-retaliation protections.
→ If your landlord violated the RLTO in how they served your notice, it may be a complete defense.
Illinois Residential Tenants Act
ILCS CHAPTER 765, ACTS 710–735
Illinois state law governing landlord-tenant relationships. Sets baseline protections for all Illinois renters — security deposit rules, habitability requirements, notice periods, and eviction procedures. Chicago's RLTO adds additional protections on top of this.
→ Applies statewide, including areas outside Chicago where the RLTO does not apply.
Illinois Eviction Act
735 ILCS 5/9-201 THROUGH 9-321
Governs the eviction (forcible entry and detainer) process in Illinois courts. Sets the legal procedure a landlord must follow — required notice periods, court filing requirements, and tenant rights during the proceeding. Many evictions fail because landlords don't follow this procedure correctly.
→ Procedural errors by the landlord can result in dismissal of the eviction case.
❓ Frequently asked questions
Common questions from tenants facing eviction in Chicago.
What happens if I ignore the eviction notice? ▾
If you ignore the notice and don't pay or cure the violation within the notice period, your landlord can file an eviction lawsuit in Cook County court. You will be served with a summons and given a court date. If you don't appear in court, the judge will almost certainly rule against you by default — meaning you can be ordered to leave. An eviction judgment on your record makes it extremely difficult to rent housing in the future. Never ignore a notice or a court date.
Can my landlord change the locks or shut off utilities to force me out? ▾
No. This is called an "illegal lockout" and it is against the law in Chicago and Illinois, even if you owe rent. Your landlord must go through the court process. If your landlord changes the locks, removes your belongings, or shuts off essential services (heat, water, electricity), call the police and contact Legal Aid Chicago or the Metropolitan Tenants Organization immediately. You may be entitled to significant damages.
What does it mean if my notice has a "procedural defect"? ▾
A procedural defect means the landlord didn't follow the legal requirements for serving the notice — for example, using the wrong notice period, failing to sign the notice, not including required language under the Chicago RLTO, or serving the notice improperly. A defective notice can be a complete legal defense, meaning the judge can dismiss the eviction case entirely. However, the landlord can usually fix the defect and start over, so use the time to contact legal aid.
I paid my rent but still got a 5-day notice. What do I do? ▾
Gather every piece of evidence you have of payment — bank statements, receipts, money order stubs, Venmo/Zelle records, text messages confirming payment. Do not ignore the notice even if you know you paid. Respond in writing to the landlord disputing the notice and stating that rent was paid in full on [date]. Keep copies of everything. If it proceeds to court, your payment evidence is your defense.
Can I be evicted if my apartment has serious repair problems? ▾
Under the Chicago RLTO, tenants have the right to withhold rent or make repairs and deduct the cost if the landlord has failed to maintain a habitable unit and has been given written notice and a reasonable time to fix the problem. However, the procedure for doing this legally is specific — you must follow it correctly or it can backfire. Contact Legal Aid Chicago before withholding rent.
How long does the eviction process take in Chicago? ▾
The timeline varies, but generally: after receiving a notice, you have the notice period (5, 10, 14, or 30 days) before the landlord can file in court. After filing, you are typically served a summons with a court date 7–40 days out. If the landlord wins, there is usually a period of at least a few days before a sheriff's eviction can be executed. The total process typically takes 6–12 weeks from first notice, but can be much faster if you miss court dates.
